What This Paradox Reveals

Job satisfaction measures the present; retention requires confidence in the future. Women enjoy their work, appreciate their colleagues, and believe in the mission. But when promotion criteria are opaque, pay bands are hidden, and advancement examples are rare, even satisfied employees update their LinkedIn profiles. The propensity to look for new opportunities isn't about dissatisfaction, it's about maximizing career potential when internal paths are unclear.

"I am proud of what I've accomplished, but growth has stalled at my company."

What Drives the Decision to Look

Among women with high propensity to explore new opportunities, the reasons are remarkably consistent, and solvable

These aren't abstract cultural issues or unsolvable problems. They're operational gaps with clear fixes: transparent pay bands, published promotion criteria, visible career paths, and genuine flexibility. Every driver below represents a competitive advantage for organizations that get it right.

Primary Factors Driving Job Search Activity

Understanding Each Driver in Detail

Pay & Benefits (44%)

Not just about amount, it's about fairness and transparency. Women see male peers promoted faster with higher comp. The opacity breeds resentment even when absolute pay is competitive.

"I would be extremely satisfied if I made more money."
"I've watched people grow... while mine seems stagnant."

WHAT WORKS: Organizations posting ranges see 30% reduction in pay-related exits. Annual equity audits with published results build trust even before gaps close.

Promotion Access (39%)

The "tap on shoulder" system advantages those in the inner circle. Women wait 1.5x longer for promotion on average, often watching less qualified men advance past them.

"There's not a lot of women in security and sometimes that influences your growth."

WHAT WORKS: Published rubrics and quarterly promotion cycles with diverse slates. Track and report time-to-promotion by demographics.

Role Frustration (34%)

Pigeonholed into support roles while men rotate through P&L positions. Limited stretch assignments and skill development create stagnation.

WHAT WORKS: Formal rotation programs, quarterly growth conversations, stretch assignments with executive visibility.

Work-Life Integration (33%)

Not about working less, it's about sustainable performance. The 191% effort requirement for women to achieve what men get at 100% creates inevitable burnout.

"I need a male counterpart to restate things for people to listen."

WHAT WORKS: Outcome-based flexibility, workload audits on high-pressure teams, recognition that values impact over hours.

Leadership Path (31%)

The Manager†’Director bottleneck is particularly acute. Women see men advance through informal sponsorship while they wait for formal processes that never come.

WHAT WORKS: Visible leadership pathways, interim roles for development, formal sponsorship (not just mentorship).

Cultural Factors

While not the primary driver, bias and exclusion erode trust in all other systems. The "good old boys" network makes even good policies ineffective.

"It is still a clique of men and very difficult to break through."

WHAT WORKS: Zero-tolerance with safe reporting, cultural audits with consequences, visible male allyship from leadership.

The Compound Effect

Death by a thousand cuts. No single barrier explains the 36% flight risk, but their cumulative weight becomes crushing. When every interaction requires extra effort, every meeting demands perfect performance, and every promotion requires extraordinary proof, even the most committed professionals reach their limits. This isn't about resilience, it's about removing unnecessary friction from the system.

Executive Summary

Momentum is real. But friction is everywhere, and it's systemic.

The Foundation is Strong

Among employed women, 87% are satisfied with their job and 59% are very satisfied. They're not just content, they're invested: 92% feel connected to the industry, 92% believe a fulfilling career is attainable, and 91% believe the field can provide financial security. 90% would encourage other women to enter the field. Together, this is a strong foundation for growth and retention.

The Paradox

Despite high satisfaction, 36% say they're at least somewhat likely to look for a new job within a year. The attitudinal battery makes the why clear: 91% say they have to work harder than men for the same promotion; 93% feel judged more on appearance; 82% believe women have fewer promotion opportunities; 81% see a "good old boys" culture.

Risk Concentration

Flight risk peaks in the 35-54 mid-career band (42% likely to look) and drops among 55+ (23%) and Under 35 (30%). Non-white women show a wider gap: satisfaction is lower (53% vs 63% for white women) and flight risk higher (41% vs 33%). The industry risks losing its most experienced talent at their peak productivity.

Manager Impact on Job Satisfaction

Manager gender matters: Very satisfied rates are 66% with male managers vs 43% with female managers—an unexpected gap that points to female managers being concentrated at smaller companies with tighter resources. They may be carrying heavier loads with less support, impacting their teams.

The Solution

Retention isn't a satisfaction problem, it's a progression problem. Women can be satisfied and still be mobile. Pay transparency, clear ladders, and equitable sponsorship are the levers that matter most. Professional development correlates with higher satisfaction, but impact on flight risk is mixed.

The bottom line: Fix how advancement works, not just how engagement feels. Mid-career is the make-or-break moment. Concentrated flight risk among 35-54 suggests targeted advancement pathways (Manager → Director → VP) will deliver outsized ROI. Convert development into sponsorship and progression, not just participation.

Key Recommendations

1. Publish Ladders + Criteria

Calibrate titles; codify what "Director" and "VP" mean across orgs. Add auditable promotion checkpoints; force evidence-based decisions.

2. Tighten Compensation Hygiene

Annual pay-equity audit; publish ranges; link progression to compensation, close the "satisfied but mobile" gap.

3. Mid-Career Lift

Rotations with P&L exposure; visible senior-forum presentations; leadership-readiness sprints with specific deliverables.

4. Upgrade to Sponsorship

VP+ sponsors for high-potentials; two material advocacy acts/quarter; measure advancement velocity.
